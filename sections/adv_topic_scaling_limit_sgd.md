# Scaling Limits of Gradient Descent

## Outline

The phrase "scaling limit of gradient descent" is overloaded. In this section it means the family of limiting descriptions obtained when the step size, batch size, data size, model size, training time, or optimizer state are scaled together. The same SGD recursion can look like gradient flow, a small-noise diffusion, a Markov chain with a stationary law, a high-dimensional deterministic risk trajectory, or a practical large-batch training rule depending on which quantities are held fixed.

1. **First separate the different meanings of scaling limit.** The basic update is discrete, but the limit may be an ODE, an SDE, a stationary distribution, a large-batch scaling rule, a high-dimensional risk law, or an infinite-width training dynamics. A good survey should always state the scaled variables: learning rate $\eta$, batch size $B$, dataset size $N$, number of updates, number of epochs, momentum/adaptive decay constants, model width, and total tokens.

2. **The SDE limit of SGD.** Mini-batch SGD can be approximated by a stochastic differential equation when the step size is small and the gradient noise has finite covariance. This is the standard diffusion-approximation line: ODE drift from the population or empirical gradient, plus a Brownian noise term whose covariance is inherited from mini-batch sampling.

3. **Constant-step SGD as a stationary Markov chain.** A different question fixes a nonzero step size and asks where the iterates spend time after transients. Near a minimum this gives Ornstein-Uhlenbeck and Lyapunov-equation approximations; globally it motivates the "SGD as approximate Bayesian inference" and "temperature" literature.

4. **Beyond Gaussian diffusion.** The Brownian SDE is not always the right effective model. Gradient noise may be anisotropic, state-dependent, aligned with curvature, generated by without-replacement sampling, or heavy-tailed enough that a Levy-type model is more appropriate than a Gaussian diffusion.

5. **Finite-step and discrete-time corrections.** Practical SGD often uses step sizes near stability thresholds, where the infinitesimal-step SDE misses important effects. Stochastic modified equations, backward-error expansions, edge-of-stability phenomena, and exact discrete covariance recursions are all ways to keep finite-step behavior visible.

6. **Sampling protocol and noise geometry.** With-replacement mini-batches, without-replacement mini-batches, random reshuffling, gradient accumulation, and distributed micro-batching do not produce the same noise. The covariance structure matters, not only the scalar variance.

7. **SDE-based explanations of the linear learning-rate / batch-size rule for SGD.** If $B$ is multiplied by $\kappa$ and $\eta$ is also multiplied by $\kappa$, the small-noise SDE keeps the ratio $\eta/B$ roughly fixed and also keeps the gradient-flow time per epoch roughly fixed. This is the clean diffusion explanation of the linear scaling rule.

8. **Explanations of linear scaling that do not rely on the SDE limit.** The large-batch practice literature often argues directly in discrete time: one large-batch update with learning rate $\kappa\eta$ can approximate $\kappa$ small-batch updates with learning rate $\eta$ if the gradient does not change too much. Warmup is needed because that approximation is weakest early in training.

9. **Breakdown of linear scaling and critical batch size.** Linear scaling is not an unlimited rule. It breaks when the learning rate reaches a stability bound, when increasing $B$ no longer reduces the number of updates to a target loss, when the schedule changes the relevant time scale, or when optimizer state such as Adam's second moment changes with batch size.

10. **High-dimensional and pretraining scaling limits.** Some recent work studies exact high-dimensional risk trajectories or empirical LLM pretraining laws. These results ask different questions from the small-step SDE: how risk curves, critical batch size, and hyperparameters scale with model size, data size, and token budget.

11. **Momentum SGD as a bridge between SGD and adaptive methods.** Momentum changes the effective learning rate, filters gradient noise, introduces a velocity state, and changes the right scaling of decay constants. It should not be treated as plain SGD with a cosmetic extra parameter.

12. **Adaptive methods: RMSprop, Adam, and AdamW.** Adam has moving first and second moments, coordinatewise normalization, bias correction, $\epsilon$, and often decoupled weight decay. Its batch-size scaling rules are therefore not the same as SGD's linear rule; SDE analyses often predict square-root learning-rate scaling with simultaneous changes to $\beta_1$, $\beta_2$, and $\epsilon$, while newer empirical work shows additional regimes.

13. **Practical large-batch adaptive optimizers.** LARS and LAMB are engineering responses to the failure of naive global learning-rate scaling at very large batch sizes. They introduce layerwise scaling so that each layer's update has a controlled size relative to its weights.

14. **Conflicting explanations and unresolved tensions.** The literature gives several mechanisms: noise as a source of implicit regularization, noise as an optimization inefficiency, large-batch gaps as update-count artifacts, stability thresholds as the key constraint, and Adam's advantage as blockwise or coordinatewise preconditioning. These explanations overlap but are not identical.

15. **A reading grid.** For each paper, record the optimizer, sampling scheme, asymptotic regime, theorem type, scaled quantities, time variable, transient versus stationary claim, empirical domain, and failure mode. This prevents mixing a rigorous small-step diffusion theorem with a practical pretraining scaling law as if they were the same statement.

## 1. What Is Being Scaled?

Start with empirical risk minimization
$$
L_N(\theta)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \ell_i(\theta),\qquad g_i(\theta)=\nabla \ell_i(\theta),\qquad \nabla L_N(\theta)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N g_i(\theta).
$$
For a mini-batch $\mathcal{B}_k$ of size $B$, plain mini-batch SGD is
$$
\theta_{k+1}=\theta_k-\eta \widehat g_k(\theta_k),\qquad \widehat g_k(\theta)=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}_k}g_i(\theta).
$$
Writing $\widehat g_k(\theta)=\nabla L_N(\theta)+\xi_k(\theta)$ separates the deterministic drift from the gradient noise. With independent sampling with replacement,
$$
\mathbb{E}[\xi_k(\theta)]=0,\qquad \operatorname{Cov}(\xi_k(\theta))=\frac{1}{B}C_N(\theta),
$$
where
$$
C_N(\theta)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (g_i(\theta)-\nabla L_N(\theta))(g_i(\theta)-\nabla L_N(\theta))^\top.
$$
For sampling without replacement, the covariance is multiplied by the finite-population correction $(N-B)/(N-1)$, so it vanishes at full batch. This small formula already explains why papers that use different sampling protocols may get different "noise scales."

There are two natural clocks. The update clock is $k$, the number of parameter updates. The gradient-flow clock is $t=k\eta$. The epoch clock is $e=kB/N$, the number of passes through the dataset. If $B$ is increased at fixed number of epochs, the number of parameter updates decreases. If $B$ is increased at fixed number of updates, the number of examples or tokens processed increases. Many apparent disagreements about batch-size scaling are really disagreements about which clock is being held fixed.

There are also several different limiting objects. If $\eta\to 0$ with fixed $B$ and time is $t=k\eta$, the noise disappears and SGD converges formally to gradient flow
$$
d\theta_t=-\nabla L_N(\theta_t)\,dt.
$$
If $\eta$ is small but nonzero, one often uses a small-noise SDE to approximate the discrete iterates:
$$
d\theta_t=-\nabla L_N(\theta_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\eta}\,\Sigma_B(\theta_t)^{1/2}\,dW_t,\qquad \Sigma_B(\theta)=\operatorname{Cov}(\widehat g_k(\theta)).
$$
For with-replacement mini-batches this becomes
$$
d\theta_t=-\nabla L_N(\theta_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\frac{\eta}{B}}\,C_N(\theta_t)^{1/2}\,dW_t.
$$
This SDE is best understood as a diffusion approximation to finite-step SGD, not as a literal nonzero-noise limit when $\eta\to 0$ at fixed $B$. In the strict vanishing-step limit, the diffusion coefficient also vanishes.

The first discipline in this topic is therefore terminological. "SGD converges to an SDE" may mean weak approximation over a finite time horizon, a stochastic modified equation that matches one-step moments, a stationary-law approximation for constant-step SGD, a central-limit theorem around a deterministic trajectory, or a high-dimensional homogenized limit. These are related, but they are not interchangeable.

## 2. The SDE Limit of SGD

The standard diffusion approximation treats mini-batch noise as conditionally mean-zero with finite covariance. The recursion
$$
\theta_{k+1}-\theta_k=-\eta\nabla L_N(\theta_k)-\eta \xi_k(\theta_k)
$$
has deterministic increment of size $\eta$ and random increment with covariance $\eta^2\Sigma_B(\theta_k)$. Over a time interval of length $dt=\eta$, Brownian noise has covariance proportional to $dt$. Matching covariances gives the coefficient $\sqrt{\eta\Sigma_B}$ in the SDE above.

The stochastic modified equation literature makes this precise in weak approximation. [Li, Tai, and E 2017](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v70/li17f.html) introduced stochastic modified equations for stochastic gradient algorithms, and [Li, Tai, and E 2019](https://jmlr.org/papers/v20/17-526.html) developed the mathematical foundations for SGD, momentum SGD, and stochastic Nesterov methods. The key point is analogous to backward error analysis for numerical ODE solvers: instead of saying the discrete method exactly follows the naive SDE, construct a continuous-time stochastic process whose expectations of test functions match the discrete algorithm to a specified order in $\eta$.

A simple first-order approximation is
$$
d\theta_t=-\nabla L_N(\theta_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\frac{\eta}{B}}\,C_N(\theta_t)^{1/2}\,dW_t.
$$
Higher-order stochastic modified equations add drift corrections involving Hessians and derivatives of the noise covariance. These corrections are usually ignored in heuristic discussions, but they matter when $\eta$ is not tiny. This is one reason "SDE intuition" can be directionally useful while still failing quantitatively in modern training regimes.

Nonconvex diffusion approximations are often used to discuss saddle escape and metastability. [Hu, Li, Li, and Liu 2019](https://doi.org/10.4310/AMSA.2019.v4.n1.a1) analyze nonconvex SGD through weak diffusion approximation and use random-perturbation theory to describe escape from local minima and saddles. The small-noise picture says that near a strict local minimum, escape times can be exponentially large in the inverse noise level, while near an unstable saddle the deterministic drift already helps escape. This gives a mathematical version of the intuition that mini-batch noise can help exploration, but it is still a small-step, model-dependent statement.

The SDE also gives a Fokker-Planck equation for the density $p_t(\theta)$:
$$
\partial_t p_t=\nabla\cdot(p_t\nabla L_N)+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\partial_{ij}\left(D_{ij}(\theta)p_t\right),\qquad D(\theta)=\eta\Sigma_B(\theta).
$$
If $D(\theta)=2T I$ is a constant isotropic diffusion matrix, the stationary density is formally Gibbs-like, $p_\infty(\theta)\propto \exp(-L_N(\theta)/T)$. This special case motivates the "temperature" language. But SGD noise is generally state-dependent and anisotropic, so the stationary law is not usually a simple Gibbs distribution over the training loss.

### What the SDE Approximation Does and Does Not Say

The SDE approximation preserves three pieces of information: the gradient drift, the scale of mini-batch noise, and the covariance geometry of that noise. It justifies simple scaling heuristics such as holding $\eta/B$ fixed, and it gives a language for discussing temperature, metastability, and stationary covariance.

It does not automatically prove that SGD generalizes because of noise. It does not imply that every training run samples from a Bayesian posterior. It also does not justify arbitrary large learning rates: the approximation assumes that one-step changes are small enough for a continuous interpolation to make sense. When training happens near a stability boundary, the discrete map can have behavior that the naive diffusion misses.

## 3. Constant-Step SGD and Stationary Laws

A separate scaling question fixes a small but nonzero $\eta$ and asks about the long-run distribution of SGD. This is natural because practical training often uses a constant or slowly changing learning rate for long stretches. In that regime, SGD is a Markov chain. Even if it does not converge to a point, it may have an approximate stationary distribution around a basin.

Near a local minimizer $\theta_\star$, approximate the loss by a quadratic:
$$
L_N(\theta_\star+x)\approx L_N(\theta_\star)+\frac{1}{2}x^\top Hx,
$$
and approximate the gradient-noise covariance by a constant $\Sigma_B$. The discrete linearized SGD recursion is
$$
x_{k+1}=(I-\eta H)x_k-\eta \xi_k,\qquad \operatorname{Cov}(\xi_k)=\Sigma_B.
$$
If the eigenvalues of $I-\eta H$ lie inside the unit disk, the stationary covariance $P=\mathbb{E}[xx^\top]$ solves the discrete Lyapunov equation
$$
P=(I-\eta H)P(I-\eta H)^\top+\eta^2\Sigma_B.
$$
For small $\eta$, the matching Ornstein-Uhlenbeck SDE is
$$
dX_t=-HX_t\,dt+\sqrt{\eta}\,\Sigma_B^{1/2}\,dW_t,
$$
whose stationary covariance solves
$$
HP+PH=\eta\Sigma_B.
$$
If $H$ and $\Sigma_B$ commute, the variance in eigendirection $i$ is approximately
$$
P_i\approx \frac{\eta\,\sigma_i^2}{2\lambda_i}.
$$
This formula is the clean local version of "temperature": larger learning rate or smaller batch size gives a wider stationary cloud, while sharper curvature reduces variance along that direction.

[Mandt, Hoffman, and Blei 2017](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/v18/17-214.html) used this local Gaussian picture to interpret constant-learning-rate SGD as approximate Bayesian inference. Under strong assumptions, the stationary covariance of SGD can be matched to a posterior covariance by choosing the learning rate, batch size, and preconditioner appropriately. This is a useful conceptual bridge between optimization and sampling, but the assumptions are restrictive: local quadratic loss, roughly constant noise covariance, stable linearized dynamics, and a particular relationship between curvature and gradient noise.

The stationary-law viewpoint should be kept distinct from the transient SDE viewpoint. A finite-time diffusion approximation asks whether the first part of the training trajectory is well approximated by an SDE. A stationary approximation asks what distribution is induced after many updates at a fixed learning rate. Learning-rate decay, early stopping, and changing batch size interrupt stationarity, so the stationary law may describe only a phase of training rather than the whole run.

## 4. Noise Is Not Just a Scalar Temperature

The simplest SDE story compresses mini-batch randomness to one number, often $\eta/B$ or $\eta(N/B-1)$. That scalar can be useful, but it hides three important facts: gradient noise is anisotropic, its covariance changes with $\theta$, and its distribution may not be Gaussian.

Anisotropy matters because escape from a basin depends on how noise aligns with curvature. [Zhu, Wu, Yu, Wu, and Ma 2019](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v97/zhu19e.html) analyze anisotropic SGD noise and argue that its covariance can be more effective than isotropic Langevin noise at escaping sharp minima. [Wu, Wang, and Su 2022](https://papers.nips.cc/paper_files/paper/2022/hash/1e55c38dd7d465c2526ae29d7ec85861-Abstract-Conference.html) relate a curvature-alignment property of SGD noise to stability and flat-minimum selection, proving in certain overparameterized settings that stable SGD-accessible minima must satisfy sharpness bounds depending on $B$ and $\eta$. These results are not just refinements of the scalar temperature story; they say the direction of the noise matters.

The sampling protocol also matters. With-replacement sampling produces independent mini-batch estimates conditional on $\theta_k$. Without-replacement sampling within an epoch introduces correlations. Random reshuffling changes both convergence rates and noise structure. [Mishchenko, Khaled, and Richtarik 2020](https://papers.nips.cc/paper/2020/hash/c8cc6e90ccbff44c9cee23611711cdc4-Abstract.html) show that random reshuffling can have different variance behavior and faster rates than with-replacement SGD in several finite-sum regimes. Therefore a paper that assumes independent mini-batches may not directly apply to the common "shuffle each epoch" implementation.

Gaussianity is also a modeling assumption. [Simsekli, Sagun, and Gurbuzbalaban 2019](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v97/simsekli19a.html) empirically study the tail index of stochastic-gradient noise and argue that heavy-tailed alpha-stable models can be more appropriate in some deep-learning regimes than Brownian diffusion. The corresponding continuous-time model is driven by Levy noise rather than Brownian motion, so it has jumps and different metastability behavior. This does not mean every SGD run is heavy-tailed, but it does show why blindly invoking a Gaussian central-limit theorem can be misleading.

The practical synthesis is that $\eta/B$ is a useful first-order scalar only after choosing a sampling protocol and deciding that the covariance geometry and tail behavior can be ignored for the question at hand. For learning-rate scaling, this simplification is often good enough to predict a first sweep. For generalization, basin selection, and instability, it is often too coarse.

## 5. Linear Learning-Rate / Batch-Size Scaling for SGD

The linear scaling rule says that when the batch size is multiplied by $\kappa$, the learning rate should often be multiplied by $\kappa$:
$$
B'=\kappa B,\qquad \eta'=\kappa\eta.
$$
In the SDE approximation with with-replacement sampling, the diffusion coefficient depends on $\eta/B$:
$$
d\theta_t=-\nabla L_N(\theta_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\frac{\eta}{B}}\,C_N(\theta_t)^{1/2}\,dW_t.
$$
Scaling both $\eta$ and $B$ by $\kappa$ keeps $\eta/B$ fixed. In epoch time, it also keeps the gradient-flow time per epoch roughly fixed, because an epoch has $N/B$ updates and each update advances gradient-flow time by $\eta$, so one epoch corresponds to time $(N/B)\eta$. After scaling, this becomes $(N/(\kappa B))(\kappa\eta)$, the same value.

[Smith and Le 2018](https://openreview.net/forum?id=BJij4yg0Z) express this through a noise scale
$$
g=\epsilon\left(\frac{N}{B}-1\right)\approx \frac{\epsilon N}{B},
$$
where $\epsilon$ is the learning rate. Holding $g$ fixed gives the same qualitative prediction: increase $B$ in proportion to $\epsilon$. [Smith, Kindermans, Ying, and Le 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.00489) then push the idea into a training recipe: instead of decaying the learning rate, one can often increase the batch size to reduce noise while preserving a similar learning curve, and one can scale $B$ with $\epsilon$ for larger-batch training.

The non-SDE explanation is more discrete and is closer to how the rule was popularized in large-scale ImageNet training. [Goyal et al. 2017](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.02677) argue that $\kappa$ sequential small-batch steps with learning rate $\eta$ can be approximated by one large-batch step with learning rate $\kappa\eta$, provided the model parameters and gradients do not change too much over those $\kappa$ small steps. This is a local linearization argument in update space, not a stationary diffusion argument. It also explains why warmup is needed: at the beginning of training, gradients change rapidly, batch-normalization statistics are unsettled, and optimizer state may be uncalibrated, so jumping immediately to the scaled learning rate can break the approximation.

These two explanations are compatible but not identical. The SDE explanation preserves a noise scale and an epoch-time drift scale. The discrete explanation approximates several small updates by one larger update. Both support linear scaling in a moderate regime, and both predict that the rule will fail when step sizes are no longer small.

## 6. Critical Batch Size and Breakdown of Linear Scaling

Linear scaling cannot hold indefinitely. The most basic obstruction is stability. For full-batch gradient descent on a quadratic with largest Hessian eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}$, the stable learning-rate range is roughly
$$
0<\eta<\frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}}.
$$
Deep networks are not fixed quadratics, but the same warning survives: if linear scaling pushes $\eta$ past the largest stable step size for the current training dynamics, larger batches cause divergence or require warmup, clipping, normalization changes, or a smaller-than-linear rule.

The second obstruction is diminishing returns in update count. A larger batch gives a lower-variance gradient estimate, but after a point it does not reduce the number of parameter updates needed to reach a target loss. This threshold is often called the critical batch size. [McCandlish et al. 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.06162) propose an empirical model of large-batch training based on a gradient-noise scale, roughly comparing the trace of gradient covariance to the squared gradient norm. When the batch size is below this scale, increasing $B$ can reduce the number of updates nearly proportionally. Above it, extra examples per update are increasingly wasted for time-to-target, though they may still improve hardware utilization.

[Shallue et al. 2019](https://jmlr.org/papers/v20/18-789.html) give an important empirical caution. Across many workloads, the effect of batch size on training steps and final performance varies substantially with model, dataset, optimizer, and tuning budget. They find that disagreements in the large-batch literature can often be explained by different metaparameter tuning and compute budgets, rather than by an intrinsic law that large batches always generalize worse. This is why critical batch size should be measured relative to a target metric, schedule, optimizer, and tuning protocol.

The third obstruction is generalization and schedule interaction. [Keskar et al. 2017](https://openreview.net/forum?id=H1oyRlYgg) argue that large-batch training can converge to sharp minimizers with a generalization gap. [Hoffer, Hubara, and Soudry 2017](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.08741) emphasize a different mechanism: large batches often receive fewer parameter updates for the same number of epochs, and training longer can close much of the gap. These papers are often presented as opposing narratives, but they can both be partly true: large batches change noise, update count, and stability all at once.

Modern stability work adds another caveat. [Cohen et al. 2021](https://openreview.net/forum?id=jh-rTtvkGeM) show that full-batch gradient descent on neural networks often operates at the edge of stability, with Hessian sharpness hovering near $2/\eta$ and nonmonotone short-term loss behavior. For mini-batch SGD, recent work on the [edge of stochastic stability](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.20553) suggests that the relevant stability quantity can involve mini-batch curvature rather than only the full-batch Hessian. This line of work is not a replacement for diffusion theory; it is a warning that finite-step stability can be the dominant constraint in practical training.

The practical rule is therefore conditional: linear scaling is a good first sweep when updates are small, the optimizer state is comparable, the batch is below the critical regime, the learning-rate schedule is retuned or warmed up, and the goal is to preserve a similar epoch-level trajectory. It is not a theorem that arbitrary larger batches should use arbitrary larger learning rates.

## 7. High-Dimensional and Pretraining Scaling Limits

Small-step SDEs are not the only mathematical scaling limits for SGD. Another line studies high-dimensional limits where dimension, sample size, and sometimes time all grow together. These results can be much sharper than generic diffusion heuristics because they exploit specific models, often least squares, random features, or kernel regimes.

[Paquette, Paquette, Adlam, and Pennington 2022](https://papers.nips.cc/paper_files/paper/2022/hash/e9d89428e0ef0a70913845b3ae812ee0-Abstract-Conference.html) study exact risk trajectories of SGD in high-dimensional quadratic problems. They derive a homogenized SGD description whose risks are characterized by deterministic Volterra equations. A key lesson is that, in their setting, SGD's benefit is better described as implicit conditioning rather than implicit regularization, and the stochasticity can hurt population risk even while improving optimization efficiency. This is a useful counterweight to broad claims that SGD noise is always a generalization aid.

For neural networks, infinite-width and mean-field limits ask yet another question: what deterministic function-space dynamics emerge as width goes to infinity? Those limits are usually about representation and optimization dynamics, not mini-batch noise per se. They interact with batch-size scaling when the model-size limit changes the curvature, gradient covariance, or learning-rate transfer rule. For example, $\mu$P-style width scaling, introduced in [Yang et al. 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.03466), is about transferring learning rates across model width; it is conceptually separate from the batch-size rule, although practical pretraining recipes must tune both.

Recent language-model pretraining papers make critical batch size even more context-dependent. [Zhang et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.21676) study auto-regressive pretraining and argue, after controlled sweeps over model size, data size, momentum, learning rate, and schedule, that critical batch size scales primarily with data size rather than model size in their setup. [Filatov, Ebert, Wang, and Kesselheim 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.05838) study optimal learning rate and batch size in an infinite-data perspective and report that the optimal batch and critical batch can evolve with training token budget. [Bergsma et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.13738) study weight decay and batch size in LLM pretraining and find power-law behavior for optimal and critical batch sizes, again tied strongly to data scale.

The synthesis is that "critical batch size" is not a universal constant of an architecture. It depends on the loss target, training stage, data size, schedule, optimizer, and definition of efficiency. In a small-step SDE, a scalar noise scale may summarize the local update. In pretraining, the relevant object is often a compute-time-token tradeoff curve.

## 8. Momentum SGD

Momentum changes the scaling problem because it introduces memory. In the common deep-learning convention,
$$
v_{k+1}=\mu v_k+\widehat g_k,\qquad \theta_{k+1}=\theta_k-\eta v_{k+1}.
$$
For a constant gradient, $v_k$ approaches $\widehat g/(1-\mu)$, so the effective step along a persistent gradient is closer to $\eta/(1-\mu)$ than to $\eta$. Momentum also filters noise: high-frequency gradient noise is averaged through the velocity state, while low-frequency components persist.

A continuous-time limit depends on how $\mu$ is scaled. If $\mu$ is fixed while $\eta\to 0$, the velocity relaxes rapidly and the limiting behavior can collapse toward first-order gradient flow with a rescaled step. If $\mu=1-\gamma\eta$, the velocity has a nontrivial time constant and the limit resembles an underdamped system. The stochastic modified equation work of [Li, Tai, and E 2019](https://jmlr.org/papers/v20/17-526.html) explicitly treats momentum SGD and stochastic Nesterov methods. [Liu, Chen, Zhou, and Zhao 2021](https://doi.org/10.1287/stsy.2021.0083) analyze diffusion approximations for momentum SGD in nonconvex optimization, including saddle escape and local convergence effects.

Momentum also modifies batch-size scaling rules. [Smith, Kindermans, Ying, and Le 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.00489) observe empirically that increasing the momentum coefficient can play a role similar to increasing batch size, with scaling behavior involving $1/(1-\mu)$. The exact rule depends on the momentum convention, whether gradients are multiplied by $(1-\mu)$ before accumulation, and whether one is matching transient trajectory, stationary covariance, or training loss per epoch.

The important point is simple: SGDM is not plain SGD with one decorative hyperparameter. Its state variable changes both drift and noise filtering, so any scaling rule should specify whether $\eta$, $B$, and $\mu$ are scaled together.

## 9. Adam, RMSprop, and AdamW

Adam is more complicated because it changes both the direction and the scale of each coordinate update. Ignoring bias correction, Adam uses
$$
m_{k+1}=\beta_1 m_k+(1-\beta_1)\widehat g_k,\qquad v_{k+1}=\beta_2 v_k+(1-\beta_2)\widehat g_k^{\odot 2},
$$
and then updates
$$
\theta_{k+1}=\theta_k-\eta \frac{m_{k+1}}{\sqrt{v_{k+1}}+\epsilon}.
$$
AdamW, from [Loshchilov and Hutter 2019](https://openreview.net/forum?id=Bkg6RiCqY7), decouples weight decay from the gradient update, typically adding a multiplicative shrinkage step of the form $\theta\leftarrow (1-\eta\lambda)\theta$.

The key batch-size complication is that Adam's denominator depends on the square of the stochastic gradient:
$$
\mathbb{E}[\widehat g_k^{\odot 2}\mid \theta]=(\nabla L_N(\theta))^{\odot 2}+\operatorname{diag}(\Sigma_B(\theta)).
$$
Changing $B$ changes not only the random fluctuation around the update direction, but also the expected second-moment estimate itself. In a high-noise regime, $v$ may be dominated by gradient variance. In a low-noise regime, it may be dominated by squared mean gradient. A scalar rule based only on $\eta/B$ cannot capture this transition.

[Malladi, Lyu, Panigrahi, and Arora 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.10287) derive SDE approximations for RMSprop and Adam and use them to propose a square-root scaling rule for adaptive methods. When batch size is multiplied by $\kappa$, their rule scales
$$
\eta'=\sqrt{\kappa}\eta,\qquad \beta_i'=1-\kappa(1-\beta_i)\quad (i=1,2),\qquad \epsilon'=\epsilon/\sqrt{\kappa}.
$$
This is very different from SGD's linear learning-rate rule. It also has explicit feasibility constraints: for example, $\beta_i'$ must remain in a valid range, and the SDE assumptions must still describe the optimizer state. The value of this result is not that Adam always obeys one universal square-root law; it is that preserving adaptive-optimizer dynamics requires scaling the moment time constants and $\epsilon$, not only the learning rate.

Adam also has convergence pathologies in simple settings. [Reddi, Kale, and Kumar 2018](https://openreview.net/forum?id=ryQu7f-RZ) show that Adam can fail to converge in a convex example because of the exponential moving average used in the second moment, and they propose AMSGrad-style fixes with longer-term memory. This result is not about batch-size scaling, but it is a reminder that adaptive normalization changes the underlying optimization algorithm in ways that are not captured by the SGD diffusion picture.

Recent empirical and theoretical work further complicates Adam scaling. [Li et al. 2024](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper_files/paper/2024/hash/ef74413c7bf1d915c3e45c72e19a5d32-Abstract-Conference.html) study a "surge" phenomenon for Adam-style sign-based optimizers: as batch size increases, the optimal learning rate can first rise and then fall, with the peak moving during training. This directly contradicts a naive monotone linear-scaling story for Adam-style methods. [Zhang et al. 2024](https://openreview.net/forum?id=X6rqEpbnj3) argue from a Hessian perspective that Transformers exhibit block heterogeneity, making one global SGD learning rate poorly suited and making Adam's coordinatewise adaptation useful. [Zhao, Morwani, Brandfonbrener, Vyas, and Kakade 2025](https://openreview.net/forum?id=zfeso8ceqr) compare optimizers for autoregressive language modeling and find that several adaptive or sign-like methods can perform comparably while SGD lags, suggesting that much of Adam's practical advantage may come from robust preconditioning and sign/momentum structure rather than an unexplained optimizer-specific effect.

Weight decay adds another scaling axis. AdamW's decoupled shrinkage depends on $\eta\lambda$ per update, so changing $B$, $\eta$, and the number of updates changes the effective shrinkage per token or per epoch. [Wang and Aitchison 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.13698) analyze AdamW weight decay through an exponential-moving-average timescale viewpoint. [Bergsma et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.13738) study LLM pretraining hyperparameter scaling and use an AdamW timescale of the form
$$
\tau=\frac{B}{\eta\lambda D}
$$
to relate batch size, learning rate, weight decay, and data size. The lesson is that scaling AdamW batch size without revisiting weight decay can silently change the regularization timescale.

## 10. Practical Large-Batch Optimizers: LARS and LAMB

When global learning-rate scaling becomes unstable, one practical response is to scale updates layer by layer. LARS, introduced by [You, Gitman, and Ginsburg 2017](https://arxiv.org/abs/1708.03888), uses a layerwise trust ratio so that a layer's update norm is controlled relative to its weight norm. This allowed very large batches for CNN training in regimes where a global linear-scaling recipe could diverge or lose accuracy.

LAMB extends this idea to Adam-style preconditioning. [You et al. 2020](https://openreview.net/forum?id=Syx4wnEtvH) propose LAMB for large-batch deep learning and demonstrate BERT training with very large batches. The algorithm combines Adam-like coordinatewise normalization with a layerwise trust ratio. Conceptually, LAMB says that even after Adam normalizes coordinates, different layers may still need different effective step sizes to keep update magnitudes compatible with parameter magnitudes.

These optimizers should not be treated as proofs of a universal scaling law. They are evidence that practical large-batch training often needs additional normalization beyond global $\eta$ and $B$ scaling. Their success also reinforces a recurring theme: as batch size increases, the limiting factor may move from gradient-estimator variance to stability, layerwise conditioning, optimizer-state calibration, or regularization.

## 11. How to Read the Literature

The literature is easiest to parse if each paper is assigned to a regime. A rigorous diffusion paper may prove weak approximation as $\eta\to 0$ over finite time. A stationary-law paper may assume a quadratic basin and constant covariance. A large-batch engineering paper may tune warmup, schedules, normalization, and regularization until a target accuracy is recovered. A pretraining scaling-law paper may fit empirical power laws over model size, data size, and token budget. These are all useful, but they do not answer the same question.

Use the following checklist when reading a scaling-limit or batch-scaling claim:

- **Optimizer:** SGD, SGDM, Nesterov, RMSprop, Adam, AdamW, LARS, LAMB, or a sign-style simplification.
- **Sampling:** with replacement, without replacement, random reshuffling, gradient accumulation, distributed micro-batches, or full batch.
- **Time variable:** updates, gradient-flow time $k\eta$, epochs, tokens, wall-clock time, or stationary time.
- **Asymptotic regime:** $\eta\to 0$, $B\to\infty$, $N\to\infty$, width $\to\infty$, dimension/sample ratio fixed, or empirical scale sweep.
- **Noise model:** scalar variance, full covariance, anisotropic state-dependent covariance, heavy-tailed noise, or no stochastic approximation at all.
- **Claim type:** transient trajectory, stationary distribution, escape time, risk curve, time-to-target, final generalization, or hardware efficiency.
- **Scaled hyperparameters:** $\eta$, $B$, $\mu$, $\beta_1$, $\beta_2$, $\epsilon$, weight decay $\lambda$, warmup length, decay schedule, and total tokens.
- **Failure mode:** instability, loss spike, critical batch saturation, worse final accuracy, changed regularization, optimizer-state mismatch, or hardware inefficiency.

## Takeaway

The SDE approximation of SGD is the cleanest mathematical starting point:
$$
d\theta_t=-\nabla L_N(\theta_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\frac{\eta}{B}}\,C_N(\theta_t)^{1/2}\,dW_t.
$$
It explains why the ratio $\eta/B$ is central and why linear learning-rate scaling with batch size can preserve a first-order SGD noise scale. But the approximation is only one lens. Finite-step stability, stationary covariance, noise anisotropy, heavy tails, sampling protocol, momentum, Adam's moment states, weight decay timescales, and pretraining compute tradeoffs all create additional scaling rules.

For plain SGD, the best concise statement is: linear scaling is a useful moderate-regime rule, usually paired with warmup, and it breaks near stability limits or beyond the critical batch size. For Adam/AdamW, the best concise statement is: there is no direct analogue of the SGD linear rule; preserving adaptive dynamics requires thinking about $\eta$, $\beta_1$, $\beta_2$, $\epsilon$, $B$, and weight decay together, and recent evidence shows multiple regimes rather than one universal law.
